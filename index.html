My research interests are mainly focused in Plant Breeding, Genetics and Plant Biotechnology. Currently I’m using genetics, high-throughput sequencing and biochemistry to decipher the molecular mechanisms that control important agronomic characteristics of plants. ​I am especially interested in the discovery and deployment of genes controlling relevant aspects of development and their utilization to produce improved ​crop varieties.

    • Biography

    ​I completed my undergraduate studies at the University of San Martin (Argentina) with emphasis in Biotechnology in 2010. The same year I joined the Dubcovsky lab at UC Davis to apply different genetic approaches to understand the regulation of the vernalization requirement in wheat. I received my PhD from the University of Buenos Aires in 2015 under the supervision of Jorge Dubcovsky. I joined Luca Comai's laboratory in November 2016 to develop new genetic tools for plant breeding.

    Research

    Developing new genomic tools for plant breeding

    Humans have modified and shaped plants to produce food, shelter and medicines since the beginning of agriculture. This activity commonly called 'plant breeding' currently aims to produce high yield, resilient and better adapted cultivars. Unfortunately, plant breeding has only been focused on a small number of crops, and some plants with complex genetic compositions were impeded to benefit from modern breeding tools. I'm currently working on the utilization of classic and modern genetics tools to initiate plant breeding and also generating new genetic resources to facilitate crop improvement.

    Temperature regulated development

    Plants sense their environment for cues to maximize the probabilities of reproductive success. In temperate cereals, a long exposure to low temperatures accelerates flowering. This response, named vernalization, is crucial to optimize seeds production in wheat and barley. Using these as model systems I'm interested in understanding the developmental networks that control the vernalization requirement in cultivated cereals.

    Early adaptation to different environments

    Since domestication, plants have been adapted to different environmental conditions. I'm interested in approaches to capture this genetic diversity and its deployment to produced crops better adapted to new environments.
